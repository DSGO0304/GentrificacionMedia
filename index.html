Gentrification in Mexico

Mexico City and Oaxaca are portrayed in articles and short videos as creative, lively, and "Instagrammable" places to visit.These pictures make places more desirable, which can speed up changes in rent, tourists, and neighborhoods.

Start with the Thesis

Thesis

This project explores how gentrification in Mexico is shaped by globalization, especially through global media, tourism and digital platforms. Neighborhoods such as Roma–Condesa, Coyoacán and Oaxaca City are turned into global lifestyle brands.

The main argument is that gentrification is not only about foreigners moving in. It is driven by economic systems, housing policies and global media that privilege people with higher buying power, often at the expense of long-term residents.

  • Global media & city branding
  • Tourism & digital nomads
  • Rents, Airbnb & displacement
  • Protest, xenophobia & social justice

Global Media & Urban Desire

Articles and short-form media frame Mexico City and Oaxaca as creative, colorful and “Instagrammable” destinations. These images amplify desirability and can accelerate rent pressure, tourism and neighborhood change.

Media Mechanisms

  • “Best neighborhood” lists and lifestyle city guides that rebrand places.
  • Influencers promoting cafés, rooftops, murals and “hidden gems.”
  • Hashtags that turn streets into Instagram landmarks.

On the Ground

  • More short-term rentals and boutique hotels.
  • Shift from local markets to tourist-oriented businesses.
  • New class and language boundaries (English menus, dollar prices).

Glocalization

Drawing on Kraidy’s idea of glocalization, this project sees these areas as hybrid spaces where global and local cultures interact. Local traditions are reshaped to appeal to international visitors, while global styles adjust to fit the aesthetics and identity of Mexican neighborhoods.

  • Global cafés use Mexican tiles, colors and Indigenous textiles.
  • Local food, art and traditions are branded for tourists and digital nomads.
  • Hybridity happens in an unequal system: people with dollars have more power.

Glocalization helps explain why culture becomes both a product and a justification for rising prices in these neighborhoods.

Case Studies

Roma–Condesa, Mexico City

  • Historic elite neighborhood that later became more middle class.
  • After rent controls ended, investors renovated buildings and raised prices.
  • Remote workers and Airbnb increased demand, displacing long-term tenants.

Oaxaca City

  • Global tourism hotspot built around Indigenous culture and gastronomy.
  • Boutique hotels and upscale restaurants replace family homes and markets.
  • Local communities see rising taxes and rents without equal benefits.

Coyoacán, Mexico City

  • Marketed globally through Frida Kahlo, art and “bohemian” branding.
  • Traditional shops struggle with higher rents and tourist-focused businesses.
  • IRReview describes this as growth at the expense of cultural erasure.

Media vs Reality: Roma, Coyoacán and Oaxaca

Global media and tourism platforms often show Mexican neighborhoods as colorful, safe and “Instagrammable.” This gallery contrasts those idealized images with more grounded views of local life, housing and everyday streets in Roma–Condesa, Coyoacán and Oaxaca City. Together, they show how gentrification is not only about architecture and design, but also about who each space is made for.

Protest, Xenophobia & Social Justice

When it becomes xenophobia

Juan’s poster is a clear example of how frustration with gentrification can slide into xenophobia. The message reflects real concerns about displacement, rising rents and Airbnb, but it shifts the blame away from housing policy and onto foreigners as a group.

Instead of pointing to landlords, platforms or the state, the poster treats “foreigners” as the main problem. This turns a structural housing crisis into a conflict between locals and outsiders and makes nationality or language the dividing line.

In that sense, the poster mixes a legitimate demand for housing justice with a xenophobic frame: it defends the neighborhood, but it does so by targeting people rather than the systems that make living there unaffordable.

Street protests & community responses

People respond to gentrification in many ways. Some protests attack foreigners directly, while others connect rent hikes and tourism to broader struggles for housing rights and community care.

How U.S. government messages framed these protests

From a globalization and media perspective, these tweets work like a small piece of global media that spreads a very narrow story. They present Mexico City as beautiful but dangerous and treat the protests mainly as a security problem for the United States, not as a reaction to rent increases, displacement and the pressure created by tourism and digital nomads.

This framing feels especially disrespectful in a context where many Mexicans migrate to the U.S. in search of safety, stability and better opportunities, while many foreigners move to Mexico because life is cheaper and more comfortable for them. By commenting on the protests only as a “risk” or as background for border policy, these official accounts ignore the inequality and history behind the marches and recycle stereotypes of chaos and danger instead of listening to local demands.

Theoretical Framework: Globalization, Media & Migration

This part brings together different points of view on how global media, migration, and state propaganda change what a place means. The four authors below help us understand why neighborhoods are "imagined" as appealing to people from other countries, why simple pictures get around so quickly, and how protest signs and actions on the street can be reframed by institutions and platforms far away.

Arjun Appadurai — mediascapes

Appadurai’s concept of mediascapes emphasizes the global flows of images, texts and audio that shape people’s imagination of places. Mediascapes explain how Roma–Condesa, Coyoacán and Oaxaca are packaged as lifestyle destinations attractive to tourists and digital nomads: curated photos, short videos and influencer narratives create desire and mobility. Applied to gentrification, mediascapes show how demand for "Instagrammable" neighborhoods is produced externally and then materializes locally as higher rents and commercial turnover. They also clarify why protest imagery is easily decontextualized when shared across borders: a single viral clip can replace structural explanations with sensational impressions.

Lechner — migration & imagined destinations

Lechner's research on migration and imagined locations shows how economic inequality and people's hopes and dreams affect where people go and how they move. People move both ways because of migration flows and the idea of "cheap, attractive" towns. Some people leave Mexico to find safety and work, and others move to. Mexican areas that were shaped by the media. This makes the housing market more competitive as more foreigners move in and change demand, and local markets have to adjust. Lechner helps us understand that gentrification is connected to larger patterns of migration and to the idea that some cities are attractive places to visit around the world.

Rane & Salem — diffusion of protests

Rane and Salem look at how protest messages get spread on social media and how groups are changed as they go online. Their point of view shows both the ability of platforms to bring people together (by recording evictions and planning rallies) and the danger of changing the way things are seen: Detached short clips, messages, or pictures get around. from their original setting and are reinterpreted by people far away. In the case of housing protests, social media can make claims for renter rights more visible while also making it easier for people outside of the protests to see them as racist or disorderly.

Saif Shahin — state narratives & digital power

A big part of Shahin's study of state players and digital power is looking at how official stories are made and shared online. This lens makes it clear why DHS or other U.S. contacts can make local protests into security issues: State words have power and influence, and they can change the way things are built accounts that specifically tell foreigners about the danger they face. I think Shahin helps to show how institutional tweets work as a type of global media that values mobility and bordered views over local accounts of displacement and speculation.

Conclusion:

Gentrification in Mexico City and Oaxaca is deeply connected to globalization, especially through global media, tourism and digital platforms. Neighborhoods are turned into lifestyle brands that attract foreigners with higher buying power, often at the expense of long-term residents. Gentrification in Mexico is about more than simply rent prices, as shown by the protest photographs, Juan's poster, and the U.S. tweets. The issue of who has the authority to represent the city is also at risk. The housing problem and the eviction of long-term residents receive less attention in global mediascapes compared to news about hip coffee shops, inexpensive rent for foreigners, and "riots" in Mexico City.

Incorporating Appadurai, Sassen, Massey, and Hall into the framework makes these images more meaningful. Inconsistent ebbs and flows of visitors, digital nomads, capital, and press coverage shape megacities like Mexico City. Those who have been displaced are frequently warned that they are reacting "too strongly," while institutions in the North observe and make remarks, perceiving demonstrations as security risks instead of demands for fair housing. The media should not publish or air these demonstrations as news. The current approach to tourism and urban development is unsustainable, and these problems should be taken as evidence of that. We can think more fairly and without prejudice about migration, housing, and the right to reside in the city if we listen to locals and try to understand why some Mexicans go north and others go south.

Possible Solutions & Local Resistance

Real solutions should not just blame people, but address the systems that create gentrification and displacement.

Policy Ideas

  • Rent caps and public registration of rental contracts.
  • Limits on the number of Airbnbs and days they can be rented.
  • Affordable housing projects in and beyond the city center.
  • Better public services in peripheral areas to reduce pressure on central neighborhoods.

Community & Digital Activism

  • Neighborhood assemblies and campaigns against unfair evictions.
  • Use of social media to document displacement and mobilize support.
  • Coalitions between long-term residents and sympathetic immigrants.
  • Framing gentrification as a class and housing issue, not a war on migrants.
